Where the company is heading

AI & Automation

Sempervox is moving to AI and cloud. Being the person who can actually use these tools — not just talk about them — is how you make yourself valuable beyond closing tickets. Since their ticket system is a Power Apps portal on Dataverse, the Microsoft Power Platform is their natural automation home.

✨Microsoft Copilot▾

Copilot is Microsoft's AI assistant. There are a few flavours — know the difference:

  • Microsoft 365 Copilot (paid add-on) — built into Word, Excel, Outlook, Teams; grounded in your company's data and respects existing permissions.
  • Copilot Chat — the web/Edge chat assistant for general questions and drafting.

Practical uses for L1 support

  • Draft a clear reply to a user from a few bullet points.
  • Summarise a long, messy ticket thread into "what's the actual problem".
  • Explain an error message or a PowerShell command you don't recognise.
  • Turn a spreadsheet of data into a quick summary or chart.
Golden rule: AI drafts, you verify. Never paste a Copilot answer to a customer without checking it — and never put sensitive customer data into a tool that isn't approved for it. Knowing this boundary is itself a valuable skill.
⚙️Power Automate▾

Power Automate builds workflows — "when this happens, do that" — across Microsoft 365 and hundreds of other apps. Two kinds:

  • Cloud flows — triggered by events (a new email, a new ticket, a form submission).
  • Desktop flows (RPA) — automate clicks and keystrokes in apps that have no proper API.

Ideas that fit their ticket portal

  • When a new ticket is created, post it to a Teams channel and tag on-call.
  • Auto-triage: set priority/category from keywords in the ticket.
  • Kick off an onboarding checklist when HR flags a new starter.
  • Send an SLA warning when a ticket is nearing its deadline.
Concepts to learn: triggers, actions, connectors, and variables. Start by reading existing flows before building your own.
🤝Copilot Studio▾

Copilot Studio is a low-code tool for building your own AI agents / chatbots. Because it sits on Dataverse — the same database behind their ticket portal — it's a very natural next step for Sempervox.

What you could build

  • An internal helpdesk bot that answers "how do I reset my password?" from your own runbooks.
  • A bot that logs a ticket in the portal from a Teams chat.
  • A knowledge assistant grounded in company documentation.

You build it from topics (conversation paths) and knowledge sources (docs, sites, Dataverse), then publish it to Teams or a website.

🧩The Power Platform, in one picture▾

Their ticket system is Power Apps + Dataverse, so it helps to see how the pieces fit:

  • Power Apps — build custom apps/portals (their ticket system).
  • Power Automate — automate the workflows between them.
  • Power BI — dashboards and reporting on the data.
  • Copilot Studio — AI agents on top.
  • Dataverse — the shared database underneath it all.

Manage it all at admin.powerplatform.microsoft.com. Understanding this stack makes you immediately more useful in an all-Microsoft shop.

🌐Hermes agent▾

Hermes (by Nous Research) is an open-source AI agent platform — a different world from Microsoft's stack. It's an AI-ops tool, not an IT admin tool.

  • Runs across many channels — Telegram, Discord, Slack, WhatsApp, Signal, email, CLI.
  • Has persistent memory, can spawn sub-agents, and do web/browser tasks.
  • Automates tasks through natural language rather than rigid flows.
Ask your dad: what does Sempervox actually want Hermes for — client automation, an internal helpdesk assistant, report generation? The answer decides what's worth learning. Microsoft's Copilot Studio and Hermes overlap in "AI agent" territory but suit different jobs.
🐾OpenClaw▾

OpenClaw (openclaw.ai) is an open-source AI assistant that runs locally on your own computer (Mac, Windows or Linux) — billed as "the AI that actually does things". Rather than just chatting, it takes real actions on your behalf.

What it can do

  • Talk through the apps you already use — WhatsApp, Telegram, Discord, Slack, Signal, iMessage.
  • Persistent memory — remembers your preferences and history and adapts over time.
  • Takes actions — browses websites, fills forms, reads/writes files and runs shell commands.
  • Self-extending skills — you can add custom skills/plugins (shared via "ClawHub"), and it can even write its own.
  • Multi-agent — run several agents at once across different machines.

The big selling point is privacy and control: "your context and skills live on YOUR computer, not a walled garden." It's free and open-source.

OpenClaw vs Hermes: these two are close cousins — both open-source, locally-run AI agents with messaging integration, memory, skills and multi-agent orchestration. If Sempervox is evaluating both, the differences will be in setup, skill ecosystem and how each is hosted, rather than the core idea.
Why the caution matters (and impresses): a tool that can run shell commands, edit files and control a browser is powerful and risky. In an MSP that touches client systems, the grown-up questions are: where does it run, what can it access, and who's watching it? Show you think about guardrails, not just capabilities, and you'll stand out.
🎯How to actually be useful with AI▾

The mindset that gets you hired and keeps you valuable:

  • Use it to learn faster — ask AI to explain errors, commands and concepts as you work.
  • Draft, then verify — let it write the first version of a reply or a flow; you own the accuracy.
  • Automate the boring, repeated stuff — that's where automation pays off.
  • Respect data boundaries — know what customer data can and can't go into which tool.
Interview gold: "I use AI to move faster but I always verify the output and I'm careful about what data goes where." That sentence shows maturity most L1 candidates lack.
